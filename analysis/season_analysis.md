The periodization loosely follows:
- **Spring (May-Jun)**: Base building, weekend MTB rides

- **Winter (Nov-Mar)**: Maintenance, indoor + ski cross-training

## What Actually Happened

### The Good

2. **Best 20-min power of 302w** (Sep 26) and **best 5-min of 328w** (Jul 10) — strong power curve suggesting good aerobic engine AND decent anaerobic capacity.
3. **August was the monster month**: 60.3 hours, 3,369 TSS, 18,340m of climbing, FTP jumped from 269 to 283w. Best 20-min hit 298w.
4. **Sep-Oct continued the build**: 61.2h and 57.2h respectively, sustained high CTL.
5. **340 hours of MTB** — ride-specific training for your goal event.

### The Concerning
1. **The crash from November onward is dramatic**. CTL went from 106.8 → 20.9 today. That's losing 80% of your fitness. Hours went from 57-61h/month to 35-41h.
2. **Power meter appears to have stopped working ~Feb 25, 2026**. No power data on any outdoor ride since then. This means you're flying blind on your hardest rides right now.
3. **FTP mysteriously dropped from 287 to 242 on Feb 8**, then was manually bumped to 260-261. Was this a ramp test? Or a Garmin glitch? This matters — if your true FTP has declined that much over winter, you've lost significant ground.
4. **61.4% plan compliance** — you completed 108 of 176 planned workouts but did 180 "extra" rides not on the plan. This suggests either the plan was incomplete, or you went off-script often. Neither is ideal.
5. **Lowest TSB of -86** on April 13 — that's dangerously deep fatigue. You were digging a massive hole early in the season.

## Key Findings & Recommendations for Big Sky Biggie 2026

### 1. You Built Fitness Too Fast, Then Lost It All

The 2025 season pattern was: **blast from April through October, then fall off a cliff**. Your CTL went from 0 to 107 in 7 months, then back to 21 in 5 months. For the Biggie in late August, you need to be at peak CTL around 85-95 (not 107 — that was overreaching).

**Recommendation**: Smoother build. Start serious structured training in April, target CTL of 85-90 by race week. Plan 3-week build / 1-week recovery cycles.

### 2. You Need Structured Intensity — Not Just Volume

Your plan had almost zero structured intervals. All your intensity came from terrain. That works to a point, but for an A-race you need:
- **2x/week threshold intervals** (2x20 or 3x15 at FTP)
- **1x/week VO2max** (4-5x4min at 120% FTP)
- **The rest easy** — true Z1/Z2, not "MTB tempo because the trail goes up"

The **sweet spot for you is 12-14 hours/week** — not the 15-19 you were doing at peak. At 49, recovery is your limiter. You proved this: your biggest TSS weeks (W14, W33, W38) were followed by forced recovery.

### 3. Fix the Power Meter Situation

You have no power data since Feb 25. For the next 5 months of race prep, you NEED power. Get the power meter fixed/replaced immediately. HR-only training at your age is unreliable — cardiac drift, temperature, fatigue, and caffeine all skew it.

### 4. The Weight Opportunity

You started the year at ~175 lbs and after a month of epic MTB riding you're down to ~163 lbs — that's already a huge win. At 163 lbs with 261w FTP = 3.53 W/kg (up from 3.07 at the start). The Biggie has ~6,000ft of climbing in 50 miles. If you can hold 160 lbs race weight and rebuild FTP to 280w, that's **3.86 W/kg** — a massive improvement on the climbs. Even at 163 lbs and 280w FTP, you're at 3.79 W/kg. That weight loss you've already achieved is worth more than any interval session.

### 5. Recovery Protocol for a 49-Year-Old

Your data shows you can do big back-to-back weekend rides (4h Sat + 4h Sun) — that's fine for endurance. But after high-intensity efforts, you need 48-72 hours of easy/off. The weeks where you rode 7-8 days straight with TSS > 700 were overreaching.

**Proposed weekly structure (16h target):**
- Mon: Z1 recovery 1h or OFF
- Tue: Threshold intervals 1.5h
- Wed: Z1/Z2 easy 1.5h
- Thu: VO2max intervals 1.5h
- Fri: Z1 recovery 1h or OFF
- Sat: Long MTB 4-5h (race-specific)
- Sun: Long endurance 3-4h (Z2 cap)
